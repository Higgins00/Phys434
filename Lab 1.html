Part B:¶

$$\text{Binomial PMF} = \left( \begin{array}{11} n \\ k \end{array}\right) P^k (1-P)^{n-k}$$

In [186]:
fig,ax = plt.subplots(8,2,figsize=(8,20))
k = np.linspace(0,20,21)
for j in range(0,8):
    ax[j][0].bar(k,stats.binom.pmf(k,(j+1)*10,.1),label = "{0:d} trials with P=.1".format((j+1)*10))
    ax[j][0].legend()
    
for j in range(0,8):
    ax[j][1].bar(k,stats.binom.pmf(k,(j+1)*10,.1),label = "{0:d} trials with P=.1".format((j+1)*10))
    ax[j][1].legend()
    ax[j][1].set_yscale('log')

The probability of getting exactly k successes in n trials with a probability of success P=.1. As there are more trials the distribution begins to closer and closer approximate a Gaussian distribution seen as the upside down parabola shape forming in the log plots on the right. Also as there are more trials the mean begins to increase. The mean approaches n times P -in this case it is approaching 10.

Part C:¶

What is the probability that a signal-free background with a Binomial probability mass distribution(Probability of success = .1) will have 5 successes after 80 trials?

In [185]:
print('Probability of 5 successes is {0:.5f}%'.format(100*stats.binom.pmf(5,80,.1)))

Probability of 5 successes is 8.89453%

Part D:¶

This just means that for experiments that are looking for discrete counts -like the number of gamma rays detected- we can find the exact probability that 10 gamma rays are expected to be detected given a particular discrete background probability mass distribution. Where for a pdf we would also have a probability of 10.1 gamma rays being detected and this would not make sense given the experiment.

Part E:¶

This makes sense because the mean is a sum of the number of counts multiplied by their respective probabilities. This results in a value that is continuous rather than discrete. Things that remain discrete are the probability of individual numbers of counts. This is because these values depend on context of the experiment, the probability of getting a certain number of counts will always be discrete because the experiment is directly measuring a binary answer to that question. The mean on the other hand is a result of averaging over all of the successes of the experiment and this number depends on how many trials there had been conducted.
