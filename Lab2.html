The graph of the log plot of the probability looks like that of a Poisson distribution. The fact that it remained a Poisson distribution after just a few convolutions makes sense conceptually because any Poisson distributed random variable that is added to another Poisson distributed variable should maintain their quality of being Poisson except for when a large number of them are summed together because then the central limit theorem states that the pmf will approach a Guassian. This can be explained mathematically as $\Large{\sum pois(\lambda_1) +pois(\lambda_2) = \sum pois(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}$

$$ = \sum \left( \frac{e^{-\lambda_1}\lambda_1^x}{x!} + \frac{e^{-\lambda_2}\lambda_2^y}{y!} \right)$$

$$= e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}\sum \left( \frac{\lambda_1^x}{x!} + \frac{\lambda_2^y}{y!} \right)$$

$$ \text{in our case x = y = k} $$

$$\text{New Poisson} = \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)^k}{k!}$$

This new added Poisson has the same function as the original Poisson $\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$ except the means of the two Poisson distributions are now summed together

I chose the Rayleigh distribution. The Rayleigh distribution is a continuous probability distribution for nonnegative-valued random variables. It is essentially a chi distribution with two degrees of freedom.

$$ f(x;\sigma )={\frac {x}{\sigma ^{2}}}e^{-x^{2}/(2\sigma ^{2})},\quad x\geq 0 $$

The signal needs to be 31% larger or 1.31 times the value of the Version 1 $5 \sigma$ value in order to be signifigant to the probability of 5sigma. For Version 1 we get that the brightness needs to be at least 10 to be considered significant, but for Version 2 we need it to be 13.1 in order to be considered significant. This can be explained by our probability plots since once we get to large values of brightness the probability drops of very steeply (that -x^2 plot in the log view). This means that our trials number is low enough that it doesn't have a large effect on the far end of the distribution and doesn't end up contributing much to changing the probability of having a signal-free event at larger brightness numbers.
